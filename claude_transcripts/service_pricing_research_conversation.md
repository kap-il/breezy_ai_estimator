What would be the hourly wage to paint a 3 bedroom apartment (10 walls 9ft by 15) in lower east side manhattan

## 🤖 Claude

**Wall Area Calculation**

You've got 10 walls at 9ft × 15ft = 135 sq ft each, so **1,350 sq ft total**.

**Typical Hourly Rates for Painters in Lower Manhattan**

Professional painters in NYC (especially Lower Manhattan/LES) generally charge in one of two ways:

**Hourly rates** typically fall in the range of **$50–$75/hr per painter** for a licensed, insured professional in Manhattan. Some higher-end outfits charge $80–$100/hr. Unlicensed/informal painters might go as low as $35–$50/hr but that comes with trade-offs on quality and liability.

**How long the job takes** is the key variable. For 1,350 sq ft of wall (assuming standard prep — no major repairs, one primer coat, two finish coats):

- A solo painter: roughly **20–30 hours** (2.5–4 days)
- A two-person crew: roughly **12–18 hours** (1.5–2.5 days)

**So the all-in labor math looks like:**

| Scenario                 | Rate                            | Hours | Total Labor |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------- | ----- | ----------- |
| Solo, mid-range          | $60/hr | ~25 hrs | ~$1,500      |       |             |
| 2-person crew, mid-range | $60/hr × 2 | ~15 hrs | ~$1,800 |       |             |
| Solo, premium            | $80/hr | ~25 hrs | ~$2,000      |       |             |

**Materials** (paint, primer, tape, drop cloths) would add roughly **$300–$600** on top depending on paint quality.

**Flat-rate comparison:** Most Manhattan painters would quote this job at roughly **$2,000–$3,500 all-in** (labor + materials), which tends to be a better deal than hourly for a straightforward job like this. Hourly billing usually favors the painter since there's less incentive to work efficiently.

If you're thinking about DIYing it, your main costs would just be materials and a weekend or two of work.

Here's what the data actually says — and you're right to question my earlier numbers. There's an important distinction to make:

**Employee wage vs. what a contractor charges you**

The *wage* a painter earns as an employee in NYC is much lower than what a contractor bills a client:

- The average salary for a painter is $25.41/hr in New York, NY according to Indeed.
- ZipRecruiter puts the average at $23.88/hr, with most painters earning between $19.18 and $26.30/hr.
- PayScale shows an average of $20.21/hr in NYC, with a high end around $47/hr.

**What contractors actually charge clients** (the rate you'd pay) is higher because it includes overhead, insurance, profit margin, etc.:

- Paintman NYC cites roughly $55/hr per painter as the average client-facing rate, with Manhattan running around $6.80/sq ft.
- Zicklin Contracting puts it at about $50/hr per painter, or roughly $5.25/sq ft, or $400/day.

**So what does that mean for your 1,350 sq ft job?**

Using the per-square-foot method (which is more standard for quoting):

- At $5.25–$6.80/sq ft → **$7,088–$9,180** … but that's for *floor* square footage of the apartment, not wall area. Your 1,350 is wall area, so the apartment is probably ~450–550 sq ft of floor space.
- At 500 sq ft floor area × $5.25–$6.80 → roughly **$2,625–$3,400** total (labor + materials).

Undertone Interiors confirms that painting a 1-bedroom in NYC runs $2,000–$4,500, and the general range is $2.50–$6.00/sq ft.

**Bottom line:** My original $50–$75/hr range for what you'd *pay* a contractor was a bit high on the upper end. The more accurate client-facing rate is **$50–$55/hr per painter**. The employee wage itself is only ~$20–$25/hr. Total job cost of $1,500–$2,500 for a 3BR is more realistic than the $2,000–$3,500 I quoted earlier, depending on prep work and paint quality.

## 🤖 Claude

### The Job: 200 sq ft Concrete Tile Patio + Propane Fire Pit

**Crew:** Owner + 2 employees, ~3–4 day job

### YOUR COST (Service Provider Side)

**Labor — what you're actually paying out:**

The average hourly wage for hardscape workers in the US is about $23.89/hr. For your crew breakdown:

- You (owner): You don't "pay" yourself hourly on the job, but your opportunity cost is real
- Employee 1 (experienced): ~$25–$30/hr
- Employee 2 (helper/laborer): ~$18–$22/hr

So raw crew wages are roughly **$43–$52/hr** combined for the two employees. Over a 3–4 day job (~24–32 hrs), that's **$1,030–$1,660 in direct labor cost** for your employees alone.

Add ~20% labor burden (workers comp, payroll taxes, etc.) per a standard construction labor burden factor of 1.2x wages, and you're at roughly **$1,240–$1,990** in loaded labor.

**Materials — your hard costs:**

| Item                                 | Provider Cost            |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------------------ |
| Concrete tile/pavers (200 sq ft)     | $400–$1,000             |
| Base gravel + sand + edging          | $200–$400               |
| Propane fire pit unit (mid-range)    | $300–$700               |
| Fire pit accessories (glass, screen) | $100–$200               |
| Misc (mortar, sealant, delivery)     | $150–$300               |
| **Total Materials**            | **$1,150–$2,600** |

Concrete tiles run roughly $5–$10/sq ft installed, making them a budget-friendly patio option. Propane fire pit units themselves typically cost $300–$950.

**Your total cost to deliver the job: ~$2,400–$4,600**

### CLIENT COST (What You Charge)

Standard hardscape markup is 1.5x–2x your costs to cover overhead, profit, insurance, and your own compensation.

**Patio portion:**

Concrete patio installation runs $4–$12/sq ft for basic or $8–$30/sq ft for decorative stamped/stained options. Concrete tile is in the mid range. At 200 sq ft:

- Basic tile: 200 × $10–$15 = **$2,000–$3,000**
- Higher-end tile with more detail: 200 × $15–$20 = **$3,000–$4,000**

**Fire pit portion:**

The cost of a propane fire pit typically falls between $300 and $950 for the unit, and professional installation runs $250–$2,200 total depending on complexity.

For a nice above-ground propane pit installed on the new patio: **$800–$1,500** all-in to the client.

**Total client price: ~$3,500–$5,500** for a straightforward job

### Summary Table

|                                | Provider Cost            | Client Price             |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------ | ------------------------ |
| Labor (loaded)                 | $1,240–$1,990           | Baked into total         |
| Materials                      | $1,150–$2,600           | Baked into total         |
| Owner comp + overhead + profit | —                       | Markup                   |
| **Total**                | **$2,400–$4,600** | **$3,500–$5,500** |

Your gross margin on this job would be roughly **30–45%**, which is healthy for hardscaping. The fire pit is almost pure margin since the unit itself is cheap and installation on a fresh patio is minimal extra work — it's a great upsell.

### The Job: Flush Wall TV Mount + In-Wall Cable Rerouting & Centralization

**Crew:** Solo owner-operator | **Time:** 2–4 hours depending on wall type and cable complexity

### YOUR COST (Service Provider Side)

**Labor — your time:**

No employees, so your only labor cost is opportunity cost. TV mount installers typically charge $75–$100/hr, and the job takes 2–4 hours. Your time investment is roughly a half-day including travel.

**Materials — your hard costs:**

| Item                                                                | Provider Cost       |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| Flush/fixed mount bracket                                           | $20–$60            |
| In-wall cable management kit (pass-throughs, grommets, wall plates) | $15–$30            |
| In-wall rated HDMI cable (10–15ft)                                 | $15–$30            |
| Power bridge kit or outlet relocation parts                         | $25–$50            |
| Misc (anchors, screws, drywall patch, paint touch-up)               | $10–$20            |
| **Total Materials**                                           | **$85–$190** |

**Other overhead per job:**

- Gas/travel: ~$15–$30
- Insurance (prorated): ~$5–$15/job
- Tool depreciation (stud finder, drill, level, fish tape, hole saw): negligible per job

**Your total cost to deliver: ~$105–$235**

This is an incredibly high-margin job. Materials are cheap, and the real value you're selling is expertise + clean execution + the client not having to deal with it.

### CLIENT COST (What You Charge)

The average homeowner spends about $251 to have a TV mounted, with prices ranging from $153 to $353 for standard installations. But that's for a *basic* mount without cable work.

For a flush mount **with** in-wall cable concealment and centralization, the price goes up:

- TV mounting with wires hidden runs $200–$500 depending on size, mount type, and wall surface.
- In-wall cord hiding typically adds $150–$300 to the base installation cost, covering drywall cuts, cable fishing, outlet installation, and patching/repainting.
- Concealment kits alone cost $69–$100, and burying cables in the wall can run $150–$250+ depending on wall type.

**What you should charge for a flush mount + full in-wall cable concealment + centralization:**

| Service Tier                                                         | Client Price          |
| -------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------- |
| Standard (drywall, simple run, 1 HDMI + power)                       | **$300–$400**  |
| Mid (longer cable run, multiple devices, patch + paint)              | **$400–$550**  |
| Premium (brick/concrete, fireplace, complex routing, multiple rooms) | **$550–$800+** |

### Summary Table

|                             | Provider Cost        | Client Price                   |
| --------------------------- | -------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Materials                   | $85–$190            | Baked in                       |
| Labor (your time, 2–4 hrs) | $0 out of pocket     | Core of the charge             |
| Travel + overhead           | $20–$45             | Baked in                       |
| **Total**             | **$105–$235** | **$300–$550** (typical) |

**Your effective hourly rate: $50–$150/hr** depending on job complexity and how fast you work. On a clean drywall job that takes 2 hours, you're looking at $100–$175/hr effective — which is one of the best margins in the handyman/AV space.

**The play here:** This is a volume game. You can do 2–3 of these per day, minimal materials, and the perceived value to the client is massive because most people are terrified of drilling into walls and fishing cables. A 90% positive review rate means you can confidently charge mid-to-upper range ($400–$500) and still win on referrals. Flat-rate pricing is the way to go — clients hate hourly for this kind of work.
